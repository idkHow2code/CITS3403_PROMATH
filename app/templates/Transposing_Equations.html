Transposing Equations



Transposing equations is an integral aspect of mathematics. This involves using the laws of algebra to re-arrange an equation. The objective here is to rearrange the equation so that the unknown variable is by itself on one side of the equals sign (=) and all the other variables are on the other side. For Example consider the equation from Newtons first law

In this example the mass (m) was being multipied by a on the LHS. The laws of algebra are such that if we are to transpose an equation what is done on one side of the equation is undone on the other side when we bring a term to the opposite side of the equals sign. Thus since (m) was being multiplied by a to bring it to the other side of the equals sign F must be divided by m.This is because no matter what is done to an equation to make a variable the subject of the equation, the equation itself must in the end still be the equivelent of what it was before the manipulation. If we substituted numbers into the equation the numbers would remain the same regardless of how the equation is transposed.

Order of operations (BIMDAS)

When transposing an equation it is important to use the correct order of operation. By order of operation we mean add, subtract, multiply, divide, squaring etc. The convention that we use in math to achieve this is we carry out operations in the following order.



1. Do things in the brackets first ie expanding the brackets


2. Do anything involving a power or a square root next


3. Carry out operations that involve either multiplication or division.


4. Lastly carry out the operations that involve addition or subtraction.



An easy acronym to remember this order of operation is BIMDAS (brackets, indicies, multiplication, division, addition, subtraction)
